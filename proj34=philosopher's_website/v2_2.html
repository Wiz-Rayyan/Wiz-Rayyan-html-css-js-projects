Cartesian Doubt

Systematic skepticism that questions the validity of all beliefs to find indubitable truth. The foundation of modern philosophy and scientific method.

Immanuel Kant

1724-1804
Categorical Imperative

Universal moral law that applies to all rational beings. Act only according to principles you could will to be universal laws.

The Universality Test: Would your action work if everyone did it?

1844-1900
Übermensch & Will to Power

The concept of self-overcoming and creating your own values in a world without inherent meaning. Embrace your power to shape reality.

The Power Effect: Transform weakness into strength.

Aristotle

384-322 BC
Golden Mean

Virtue lies in finding the perfect balance between extremes. The path to flourishing through moderation and practical wisdom.

The Balance Effect: Find virtue through the middle way.

Jean-Paul Sartre

1905-1980
Radical Freedom

"Existence precedes essence" - You are condemned to be free and must create your own meaning through authentic choices.

The Freedom Effect: You are radically responsible for who you become.

Plato

428-348 BC
Theory of Forms

Reality consists of perfect, eternal Forms that our material world imperfectly reflects. True knowledge comes from understanding these ideal Forms.

The Cave Effect: Escape the shadows to see true reality.

René Descartes

1596 - 1650

Cartesian Doubt

Descartes' method of systematic doubt sought to establish a firm foundation for knowledge by questioning all beliefs that could possibly be doubted. His famous conclusion "Cogito, ergo sum" (I think, therefore I am) became the first principle of his philosophy.

"I think, therefore I am."

Key Contributions

  • Father of modern philosophy
  • Developed Cartesian coordinate system
  • Mind-body dualism
  • Foundationalism in epistemology
