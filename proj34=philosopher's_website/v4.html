Jean-Paul Sartre

Radical Freedom

"Man is condemned to be free"

Sartre argues that humans are absolutely free, thrown into existence without predetermined essence. We are "condemned to be free" because we cannot escape the burden of choice. Every moment, we create ourselves through our decisions. Even in extreme circumstances, we retain the fundamental freedom to choose our attitude and response. Bad faith occurs when we deny this freedom, pretending we have no choice. The anguish we feel when confronting major decisions proves our radical freedom—we are the authors of our own existence, responsible for creating meaning in an absurd universe.

Pierre-Simon Laplace

Scientific Determinism

"Given the position of all particles, the future is as determined as the past"

Laplace envisioned a universe where every event, including human decisions, follows from prior causes according to natural laws. If an intelligence could know the precise location and momentum of every atom, it could predict the entire future and retrodict the past. Human behavior, emotions, and choices are simply complex interactions of matter following physical laws. What we experience as "free will" is merely our ignorance of the countless factors determining our actions. Science reveals an orderly cosmos where apparent randomness reflects incomplete knowledge, not genuine freedom.

René Descartes

Rationalist Method

"I think, therefore I am"

Descartes championed reason as the primary source of knowledge, arguing that our senses deceive us while rational thought provides certainty. Through methodical doubt, he stripped away all uncertain beliefs until reaching the indubitable foundation: the thinking self. Mathematical truths, known through reason alone, provide the model for all genuine knowledge. Innate ideas exist in the mind prior to experience. The external world's existence can be proven through rational argument, not sensory evidence. Clear and distinct perceptions, grasped by the intellect, offer the only path to truth.

David Hume

Empirical Skepticism

"All knowledge derives from sensory experience"

Hume argued that all meaningful ideas trace back to sensory impressions—we cannot have concepts without corresponding experiences. Reason alone cannot establish matters of fact; it can only analyze relationships between ideas. Even causation, which seems fundamental, is merely constant conjunction observed through habit. We cannot rationally justify induction or prove the external world's existence. The self is just a "bundle of perceptions" with no substantial core. Miracles violate natural regularities established through experience, making them inherently improbable.

John Stuart Mill

Individual Liberty

"The only purpose for which power can rightfully be exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others"

Mill's harm principle defines the boundary between individual freedom and social authority. Society may only restrict individual liberty to prevent harm to others. Self-regarding actions—those affecting only the actor—remain beyond legitimate social control. Individual diversity and eccentricity benefit society by fostering innovation and preventing stagnation. The "tyranny of the majority" threatens individual rights as much as political despotism. Free speech, even offensive speech, must be protected because truth emerges through competition between ideas, not suppression of dissent.

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel

Social Recognition

"Self-consciousness exists in and for itself when, and by the fact that, it so exists for another"

Hegel argued that individual consciousness emerges only through social recognition—we become selves through interaction with others. The master-slave dialectic shows how self-consciousness develops through struggle for recognition. Freedom is not individual license but participation in rational social institutions. The state represents the highest form of ethical life, where individual and universal interests coincide. True freedom means willing what is rational, not arbitrary choice. Individual fulfillment requires embedding oneself in social roles and relationships that embody universal values.

Viktor Frankl

Will to Meaning

"Those who have a 'why' to live, can bear with almost any 'how'"

Frankl, drawing from his Holocaust experience, argued that the primary human drive is the search for meaning, not pleasure or power. Even in the most extreme suffering, we retain the freedom to choose our attitude and find meaning. Meaning cannot be given but must be discovered by each individual through three sources: creative values (what we give to the world), experiential values (what we take from the world), and attitudinal values (the stance we take toward unavoidable suffering). Logotherapy helps people discover their unique meaning, making life worth living even in difficult circumstances.

Albert Camus

The Absurd Condition

"There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide"

Camus confronted the absurd—the fundamental disconnect between human desire for meaning and the universe's silent indifference. We are like Sisyphus, condemned to roll a boulder up a mountain eternally. The absurd cannot be resolved through reason, escape, or hope; it must be fully acknowledged. Suicide represents philosophical defeat—the inability to live with absurdity. Instead, we must revolt against the absurd by living fully despite meaninglessness, creating our own values without transcendent justification. We must imagine Sisyphus happy, finding joy in the struggle itself.

John Rawls

Justice as Fairness

"Justice is the first virtue of social institutions"

Rawls proposed that just institutions are those we would choose from behind a "veil of ignorance," not knowing our place in society. This thought experiment yields two principles: equal basic liberties for all, and economic inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged. The difference principle permits inequality only when it improves the position of the worst-off members of society. This approach prioritizes fairness over efficiency, ensuring that social cooperation benefits everyone, especially the most vulnerable. Natural talents are morally arbitrary; their fruits should serve the common good.

Robert Nozick

Entitlement Theory

"From each as they choose, to each as they are chosen"

Nozick defended libertarian justice based on individual rights and voluntary transactions. Holdings are just if acquired through legitimate means: original acquisition from nature, transfer from willing parties, or rectification of past injustices. Redistribution violates individual rights by forcing people to work for others' benefit. Even if we could design a perfectly equal distribution, it would immediately become unequal through voluntary exchanges. Patterned theories of justice inevitably require continuous interference with liberty. People deserve what they earn through their talents and efforts.
